Why “Eleven”?

The name doesn’t mean anything. Except it kind of means everything.

Eleven started life as “lchat”, named after the first initial of the kid who inspired the idea. L turned into El, a girl you might know from Hawkins, Indiana. Eleven. Turns out the name was right for more reasons than we planned.

She tore the lab down

Somewhere in a lab, a girl got turned into an experiment against her will. She came out morally grey: real power to do good, real damage done along the way. In the end she turned that power on the people who made her.

Sounds familiar, honestly. Our group chats grew up in a lab too, raised by feeds, ads, and companies that read the room because the room was theirs to read. Eleven points that same kind of power (yes, the AI everyone’s talking about) the other way: at getting your conversations out of there. Walk your friends out of the lab. You won’t even need a nosebleed.

Thesis eleven

Karl Marx once numbered eleven short notes to himself, working out where philosophy had gone wrong. The last one ended up on his tombstone.

The philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point, however, is to change it.

We’re not trying to start a revolution. But we don’t think much of companies that talk about privacy instead of building it. Your messages are encrypted before they ever leave your phone. We don’t hold the keys. Hand the server over to whoever you like, and all they get is noise.

Thesis eleven of eleven. He was picking a fight with a philosopher named Feuerbach. We’re picking a fight with WhatsApp.

This one goes to eleven

“Why don’t you just make ten louder?” The world didn’t need another messenger. Ten was already plenty loud. We built one anyway, because some things deserve to exist whether or not they’re logical, and this was one of them. Where do we go from the most private messenger we could build? Nowhere. Ours goes to eleven.

In memory of Rob Reiner, who knew it’s such a fine line between stupid and clever.

Elevenses

Hobbits eat seven meals a day. The best one is the small one mid-morning: unhurried, unimportant, shared with whoever’s around. That’s what a group chat should feel like. Not a feed. A table. Eleven is elevenses, the little meal where nobody’s performing and nobody’s selling you anything.

And notice who the heroes actually were. Out of all the wizards, kings, and elves who’d seen three ages, the story got carried by the small, ordinary ones. Even they came out morally grey: Frodo failed right at the fire, and the quest got saved anyway, by pity and by a friend who wouldn’t leave him. Small, imperfect company, getting there together.

And if your table is more of a kitchen than a hobbit-hole: yes, chef. Every second counts.

How much more private could it be?

None. None more private.

She’s still a kid

Eleven, the one from the lab, is a kid. So is the one this app is named after. This is built so an actual kid could use it safely: a grown-up runs the chat and decides who’s in it, the only way in is an invite from someone already inside, and there’s no feed, no follower count, no strangers wandering through. Nothing here is built to keep anyone scrolling. Just the people you’d actually let through your front door.

Under parental supervision, of course. Even Eleven had Hopper.
